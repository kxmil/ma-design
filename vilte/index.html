Icelandic bio material research

Yes! The core of this research is Hemp (Cannabis Sativa L) and yes, since 2020, growing industrial hemp is officially allowed in Iceland!

Cannabis Sativa L - is a flowering annual plant and one of the earliest known cultivated plants. Now this multipurpose crop is cultivated all over the world, all parts of hemp can be potentially usable for the production of many different commodities with industrial interest from the agric ultural to food and feed, cosmetic and building. Indeed, from this highly versatile plant, it is possible to obtain various products such as fiber and shives; bio - building and thermal insulated materials; seeds, flour and oil with important nutritional and functional features; and bio active compounds of pharmacological interest.

Even though all parts of hemp can be used, in this research I focus only on usability of hemp stem shives.

The hemp stem can reach a height of more than four meters when the plant has good growth conditions. The outside of the stem is covered with bark, also called epidermis. Inside the hemp stems are bast fibers and the woody core, called shives.

By mixing hemp shives with water and lime, we get a building material called hempcrete.

Hempcrete has a range of desirable thermal, structural and moisture - handling properties that make for an excellent building insulation material. Depending on the mix variables, hempcrete c an be used as roof, wall and/or slab insulation

The main goal of this project is to find local Icelandic biomaterial compounds that will reduce the use of lime in the hempcrete mix, since lime is not a local natural resource in Iceland.

So lets take a l ook what biomaterials Iceland offers to reduce lime usage in hempcrete mixture.

VOLCANIC MATTER

Iceland is a volcanic island, formed by eruptions caused by the sea floor spreading in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean. It sits on the convergent rim, with t he plate boundary running right through the island. The plates move from here to the east and to the west, continually increasing the (geographic) distance between Europe and America. The space is filled by basaltic lava coming up from the Earth's mantle. As a result, there are volcanic rocks like basalt and tufa, and sedimentary rocks, formed by erosion of volcanic rocks. Especially the large glaciers of the island produce a decent amount of moraine. However, there are no soluble rocks like limestone or gy psum, which are formed as biogen sedimentary rocks. There are no reefs so far to the north, which would produce reef limestone.

Limestone is not found in Iceland, though you will find something called Icelandic spar, a transparent variety of calcite, or c rystallized calcium carbonate, in Reyðarfjörður fjord near East Iceland. In fact, only 8 - 10% of the rock in Iceland is sedimentary, the classification of rock that includes limestone.

MARINE MATTER

Another object of research in the search for binding ma terials is seaweeds – marine algae. The Icelandic coast is a rich resource of many edible and industrially usable seaweeds. Since the settlement of Iceland, several species have been used for human consumption, as animal fodder, as manure and for different other purposes.

As photosynthesising organisms, seaweed absorb carbon dioxide from their surroundings, as oxygen and energy are formed with the help of sunlight. Recent studies show that seaweed cover 2% of the Earth’s surface but are thought to absorb up to 70% of global emitted CO 2 . Additionally, seaweed play a huge role in Silica, Phosphorus and Nitrogen cycles, along with breaking down natural compounds which other organisms are able to use in chemical compounds and processes. In this case I am inves tigating different seaweed species that produces the most natural glue that could be used as biomaterial mixture binder.
